#### **1\. ‚ÄúData visualization is not your creative outlet; data visualization is making data understandable.‚Äù**

[rachel binx](https://medium.com/@rachelbinx) from NASA‚Äôs Jet Propulsion Laboratory eloquently described every data viz practitioner‚Äôs struggle: compromising between art and utility.

Visualization literacy and data literacy take time. In fact, [Tony Chu](https://medium.com/@tonyhschu) taught us how data visualization is the use of _space_ to control _time_ (spacetime pun?).

Intuitive data exposition is constrained by attention‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääwhat Daniel Kahneman, author of [_Thinking, Fast and Slow_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thinking,_Fast_and_Slow)_,_ would call our System 2 thinking.

Tony advocates using techniques like animation and pacing (think [scrollytelling](http://www.r2d3.us/visual-intro-to-machine-learning-part-1/)) to feed bite-size bits of information (pun intended) to those digesting our visualizations, while Rachel focuses on using data viz to render tools instead of to tell stories or paint a picture.

#### **2\. Yet, so much creativity is involved in finding solutions to visualizing complex data problems.**

Every speaker probed the realms of creativity in their mesmerizing visuals and approaches to technical rigor. [Mariko Kosaka](https://medium.com/@kosamari) transformed polynomial functions into ‚Äúpixel playgrounds‚Äù and brought kernel convolution to life with a ‚Äúpixel social graph.‚Äù

[Shirley Wu](https://medium.com/@sxywu) [abused the force](http://vallandingham.me/force_talk/#0) (d3‚Äôs force-directed graph layout) in a number of ways to simplify [Illumio](https://medium.com/@illumio)‚Äôs Illumination product ([Shirley‚Äôs slides](http://slides.com/shirleywu/building-data-visualizations-for-product#/) distill this complex idea into beautiful, understandable simplicity).

[Amelia McNamara](https://twitter.com/AmeliaMN) sneakily taught us some statistics‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääparticularly, a creative solution to distinguishing real patterns in data from randomly generated data.

[Nicky Case](https://twitter.com/ncasenmare) cleverly applied systems thinking to real-world phenomena like disease proliferation, ecosystem regeneration, and racial segregation. Nicky employed emojis in simulations to show that the world is not linear; it‚Äôs loopy. (Side note: emojis made a record-breaking number of appearances in this year‚Äôs talks.)
